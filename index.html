Project Goals¶

I will be comparing the official USDA consumer price statistics for fruits and vegetables to official US inflation rate statistics, along with other related statistics such as yearly salary. This is to visually display in one place many of these issues that personally affect everyone currently living in the country, and potentially provide enough information to be able to draw conclusions from to answer pressing questions about the state of the American economy.

This first set of data is from the USDA and includes collected average conumers prices for various goods separated by year.

import zipfile
import io

for zip_url in zips:
    urld = urljoin(base_url, zip_url)
    print(urld)
    rd = requests.get(urld, stream=True)

    #same for the Zips, extract then write.
    with zipfile.ZipFile(io.BytesIO(rd.content)) as z:
        for file in z.namelist():
            if file.endswith(".csv"):
                outfile = os.path.join("./", file.split("/")[-1])
                z.extract(file, path=outfile)

Milestone 1 Writeup: Thoughts, Challenges, and Future Plans¶

This is mostly a proof of concept about comparing official rates of inflation, the actual prices of important consumer goods, and average salaries. I haven't yet decided on which dataset to get the salaries or rates of inflation from, so I just grabbed some numbers from the Minneapolis Fed temporarily. It's a common complaint that the cost of living is increasing while wages are not keeping up, and my main goal is to collect statstics to show some data related to those pressing issues and provide a means to answer complaints such as "Things are getting more expensive but I'm not making more money".

So far the biggest challenge has been the formatting of the USDA website and actually getting the data out of it, which shouldn't be much of a surprise. The csvs don't actually end in .csv, but rather .csv?ver=(number) which presented an extra challenge. The data is also fairly limited and only exists for a select few years, so I will likely have to supplement it with other sources of consumer goods prices. It is, however, well formatted within the csvs themselves and easy to work with once read into a dataframe.

For the immediate future, I will have to decide on which inflation rate and salary data sets to use, and work with the rest of the USDA data as for this example I only took two of the years as a proof-of-concept. The first thing that sticks out is that, for the 2020-2022 example, the rate of inflation is nearly identical to the rate of increase of basic fruits and vegetables, which shouldn't be surprising but was for me. Many common complaints are usually directed at fast food restraunts and food companies selling processed food, not necessarily wholesale fruit and vegetable sellers. It might be worth it to compare the increase in cost of fruit and vegetables to the incease in the cost of a Big Mac, as well, to see if they are changing at different rates.

Milestone 2 Writeup: More Data, Models, and the Future¶

There are a few other potential things I could include to futher the point like Healthcare costs or more niche markets like insurance but all the numbers are going to generally say the same thing anyway and the point has already been made. The biggest challenge is going to actually be coming up with some kind of model. The most probable would be showing some kind of correlation between some of these data sets. Maybe income is increasing at the same rate but at a much smaller scale? There isn't much to predict or classify so that limits a lot of the possibilities.
